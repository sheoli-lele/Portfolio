## Ethics Bowl: Regional and National Champion

My two-person team won the IEB Ethics Bowl Regional Championship in early November 2025, coming out at the top of 20+ teams, and beating the University of Pennsylvania and Yale University teams in the final two rounds. Among the case topics we were asked to research and discuss, here were the ones that most excited me: 

- The present and future rights of non-biological (digital) entities
- Media ethics: the spread of misinformation and the ethics around removing someone's voice
- Ethical uses of autonomous systems in foreign policy, and the extent to which the public should get a say in these uses


[Read about our win](https://www.prindleinstitute.org/news/iebonline-25-recap/).

I was also in charge of research and prep for my team's sub-group who won the Association for Practical and Professional Ethics (APPE) National Championship. [Read about my team's win](https://www.appe-ethics.org/appe-ieb-national-competition/).

## Technical Background

My interest and experience in critical thinking and ethical reasoning is only half the story; I am also a student of Data Science and Statistics. I think of this technical background as foundational to my ethics and critical analysis work.

In the past year, I learned the ins and outs of High-Performance Computing (HPC) using Python and Bash, and can now design and execute complex data pipelines on high-performance clusters. 

Along with three peers, I built a machine learning pipeline that converts raw, unstructured time-series data into clean and structured cognitive classifications. I did so on an HPC cluster that predicts a pilot's cognitive state (focused, distracted, startled, etc.) from real-time physiological data (like EEG, heart rate, breathing, and skin conductance). I handled data cleaning, feature engineering, and model optimization across 17 pilots. [See the repository for yourself](https://github.com/vkalithkar/Flight-Crew-Analytics).

## Writing on AI Policy & Ethics

Over the past few months, I have begun to record my ideas on the policy and ethical implications of AI tools in regular blog posts. Each piece is thoroughly researched and put out as a starting point for discussion.

#### The Alignment Problem

This piece is about the Alignment Problem. I introduce the problem—which many AI skeptics do not realize is the term capturing one of their biggest worries about the technology—and offer a unique perspective on it. [Read the piece](https://substack.com/home/post/p-192360219).

#### The Turing Piece

In another post, I argue that despite our lack of understanding about complex neural networks' internal mechanisms (which we call "the black box problem"), there are risks associated with our putting off this pursuit of a deeper understanding, including alignment problems. I review Alan Turing's paper that introduced what is known as the "Imitation Game," a game built on an assumption (of "functionalism") that I reject. I think purely "functionalist" attitudes toward AI systems are harmful, as these systems cannot replace human editorial judgment in nuance-heavy environments [Read this piece](https://substack.com/home/post/p-199429839).

#### State of AI Briefs

In order to keep up with the latest developments in AI, I have started writing brief "State of AI" documents once or twice a week. Each brief serves as a jumping-off point for further research, rather than an end in itself.

[See the repository for yourself](https://github.com/sheoli-lele/State-of-AI).

## Teaching and Technical Communication

In my free time, I teach mathematics (mostly trigonometry, calculus, and statistics). While this may seem like a strange thing to include in a portfolio, I'm including it to discuss the value I get from teaching material to those struggling. Learning the fundamentals of material I was shown 5–6 years ago has closed many gaps in my understanding. FAR more importantly, it has raised new questions (e.g. How does the "+C" matter so much in indicating that antiderivatives form a family of parallel curves, which by definition have different y-intercepts?) for me to investigate.

This ethos of breaking down technical fundamentals to their simplest is important to how I view modern journalism and media ethics. In a time where AI systems are inextricable from public infrastructure, journalistic judgment requires more than just an understanding of policy; it demands a literacy of underlying technical systems!
